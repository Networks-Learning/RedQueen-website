An Online Algorithm for Smart Broadcasting in Social Networks

WSDM, 2017; arxiv.org/abs/1610.05773

Introduction

User attention is becoming a sparse commodity. This is evinced by the proliferation of studies which point at information overload or at the plethora of articles proclaiming to recommend when to post your messages to maximise the probability of your followers reacting to the post.

We will look at one broadcaster who wants his posts to gain more user attention and see how he can regulate his posting to do that.

Feeds in social media

Feeds in social media display posts of all followees of the user in some order. However, the user does not pay equal attention to all posts. Instead, more attention is given to the posts on top of the feed while the posts which disappear underneath the fold are given very little attention.

Hence, a heuristic approach to seeking user attention would be to minimize the rank (\( r(t) \)) of our posts on the follower's feed. The rank \( r(t) \) is the position of the latest post by a broadcaster on a follower's feed.

If the feeds are inverse-chronologically sorted, then the dynamics of the rank \( r(t) \) of the broadcaster in the feed can be modeled using the following equation: Our method can generalize to any ordering of posts as long as we can reverse-engineer the algorithm and write out the dynamics of \( r(t) \). $$ r(t+dt) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ccccl} & (r(t)+1) & {\color{other} dM(t)} & {\color{us} (1-dN(t))} & \text{Increases by one}\\ + & 0 & {\color{other}(1 - dM(t))} & {\color{us} dN(t)} & \text{Becomes zero}\\ + & r(t) & {\color{other} (1 - dM(t))} & {\color{us} (1- dN(t))} & \text{Remains the same} \end{array} \right. $$ where \( {\color{other} dM(t)}, {\color{us} dN(t)} \in \{0, 1\} \) denote the presence or absence of a broadcast in the respective point process at time \( t \).

The evolution of \( \color{us} N(t) \) is under the control of the broadcaster, whereas the \( \color{other} M(t) \) may behave in various ways: Karimi et. al. assume that the broadcasting frequency of other broadcasters is piecewise constant.

  • It may be nearly constant (e.g. large number of diverse followees)
  • It may have bursts (e.g. breaking news posts)
  • It may be piecewise constant with some period (e.g. most followees are from a particular time zones)
  • \( \ldots \)

Thanks to our novel formulation of the problem, our solution RedQueen is able to handle all of these cases, without needing to infer the parameters of any process.

Objective

Now one can attempt to optimize for many different kind of losses to find our optimal rate of posting. One such choice, taken by Karimi et. al. is to optimize for Time spent in top-k, wherein, they find the rates which maximize the time the broadcaster's posts spend with \( r(t) \lt k \). However, we choose the following quadratic loss function: This objective can be generalized for multiple followers. For details, see our paper. $$ \begin{align} \underset{u(t_0, t_f]}{\text{minimize}} & \quad \mathbb{E}_{(N, M)(t_0, t_f]}\left[ \int_{t_0}^{t_f} \left( \frac{1}{2} s \,r^2(\tau) + \frac{1}{2} q\,u^2(\tau) \right) d\tau \right] \\ \text{subject to} & \quad u(t) \geq 0 \quad \forall t \in (t_0, t_f] \end{align} $$

This function penalizes high ranks quadratically \( \frac{1}{2} s \,r^2(t) \) while also containing the term \( \frac{1}{2} q\,u^2(t) \), which trades off posting with a high frequency and maintaining a low rank.

The \( s \) and the \( q \) denote the relative penalty of the rank we obtain and the amount of broadcasting we do. The term \( s \) can be made a function of time, i.e. \( s(t) \), to change the significance of obtaining a high rank at time \( t \). Also, in case of multiple followers, the term \( s \) can be a vector, which can be used to weigh the ranks of the broadcaster on different follower's feeds differently. For details, see our paper.

With this choice of the loss function, the optimization problem can be solved analytically. We arrive at the elegant solution: $$ \overbrace{u(t)}^{\text{broadcast intensity}} = \sqrt{s/q}\,\underbrace{r(t)}_{\text{Rank in feed}} $$

This function states that the rate at which the broadcaster must post messages (i.e. the urgency of posting), should be proportional to how bad his rank \( r(t) \) is. Hence, he will have to post faster to stay where he original was (i.e. at the top).

Determining the rate requires only knowing the rank of the broadcaster on the feed(s) of his follower(s). This information can be calculated in \( \mathcal{O}(1) \) time, given the stream of posts from other broadcasters, the sampling, as shown below, too takes only \( \mathcal{O}(1) \) time, making this an efficient online algorithm for determining when-to-post.

The Sampling

Now that we know what how the broadcaster should regulate his messages, we have to determine when exactly should he post.

However, thanks to the superposition property of Poisson point processes we can treat each jump as the start of a new Poisson process.

Now to draw the time of first event for \( u(t) \), i.e. when our broadcaster posts, we can draw samples from all point processes and take the minimum.

Since drawing one sample is an \( \mathcal{O}(1) \) operation, and we draw one sample each time other broadcasters post, we only have to take \( \mathcal{O}(M(t_f)) \) samples.

Results

We compared RedQueen against an Oracle and the state of the art and saw that our algorithm fared favourably. Tests were also conducted using real tweets from Twitter and over 2000 users. RedQueen was able to obtain better average rank as well as higher time in the top-1 compared to the real users as well as against the current state of the art. For details, see our paper.

The Name RedQueen

The name is taken from the Red Queen's race incident in Lewis Carroll's book Through the Looking Glass. In particular, the Queen's quote:

"Now, here, you see, it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place."
